Unit 9

This unit centred on using Excel to visualise data effectively through bar charts, clustered column charts, and histograms. Although these charts may appear straightforward, constructing them carefully highlighted the importance of clarity, accuracy, and design choices in communicating data insights—skills that are vital for both academic and professional analysis.

To explore brand preferences across demographic areas, I used bar charts to visualise breakfast cereal data. Starting with Area 2, I built a percentage bar chart and then compared it to Area 1. Although the numerical differences were subtle, the visuals made patterns much clearer—for example, higher relative preference for Brand A and Brand B in one region. Creating a combined clustered column chart further clarified these differences by placing both datasets side by side, offering a more immediate comparison.

The next stage involved working with continuous data through relative frequency histograms. I focused on weight loss outcomes for Diet B, setting appropriate class intervals and calculating relative frequencies before plotting the chart. This process helped visualise the distribution’s shape, making it easier to spot symmetry or skewness. Repeating the same for Diet A highlighted the comparative differences between the two diets more effectively than tables or averages could.

This visual approach required careful application of spreadsheet logic and chart formatting skills. It wasn’t just about building graphs, but about turning data into something readable and insightful.

These exercises built on techniques I had first encountered in the Numerical Analysis module. They pushed me to refine how I present data visually—an ability I expect will be essential when interpreting model outcomes and communicating findings in my MSc project.
